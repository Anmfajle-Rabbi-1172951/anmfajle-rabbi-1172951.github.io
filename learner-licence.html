How I Passed My NZ Learner Licence on the First Attempt

Christchurch, New Zealand

I had never held a driving licence back in Bangladesh. So when I moved to New Zealand, getting my Learner Licence became one of my first real goals — something to tick off before my first semester even ended.

Getting Started. My first stop was the AA centre in Riccarton, Christchurch. I picked up the official Road Code book and a practice exam booklet ($50), along with the hard copy learner application form. Before doing anything else, I just sat and watched — how other people were going through the process, what the real exam actually looked like from the outside.

Learning the Rules, Not Just the Answers. I read through the full Road Code, then went back and revised the parts I found tricky, making sure I actually understood them instead of memorising them. For hands-on practice, I followed the official drive.govt.nz/learner-licence site closely — it has the full interactive Road Code plus chapter tests, and I created an account there to track my progress. I went through all 12 learner modules and did the practice exams repeatedly, alongside the exam MCQ booklet I'd bought from AA. Between the two, I felt like I'd seen almost every type of question the real test could throw at me.

Watching Real Traffic. Books only take you so far. Whenever I was out and about on the Metro bus, I'd watch the driver closely — what rules they followed, how they handled turns, give-ways, roundabouts. If something didn't make sense, I'd ask my Kiwi roommate to explain it. I also spent time just sitting near the roundabout on Gerald Road, Lincoln University, watching how real drivers gave way to each other. That one habit cleared up more confusion than any textbook page.

Booking the Test. Once I felt properly ready, I went back to AA and booked the test for $100. That same day I also did my eye test and photo — but by then, the centre was closing for the day. They were flexible about it though, and told me to come back the next morning at 11 AM with my original passport and student ID.

Test Day. The next morning, I turned up at AA Riccarton with my documents. They gave me a password to log into the test computer, and once I was in, I followed the instructions and started answering the multiple-choice questions one by one. After each answer, a green tick confirmed if I was right. Whenever a question confused me, I skipped it and moved on — no point getting stuck.

By the time I'd confidently answered 32 questions, I relaxed. I went back to the 2 I'd skipped, worked through them, and finished the whole test in about 12 minutes.

Result: Passed. They handed me my temporary paper Learner Licence right away. My physical card arrived by mail about two weeks later.

The Plan Behind It. This wasn't random — it was timed. I'd decided during my mid-semester break (first semester) that I'd get my Learner sorted then, so that six months later I'd be eligible to sit my Restricted Licence. One small step, properly planned and properly executed.

Key Takeaways for Anyone Starting Out

1. Don't just memorise — understand. Right-of-way and give-way rules at roundabouts trip up most people.

2. Use the official practice tool. drive.govt.nz/learner-licence has the real Road Code and practice tests — I relied on it completely.

3. Watch real traffic. Sitting near a roundabout and observing real drivers taught me more than the book alone.

4. Skip and return. If a question stumps you, move on and come back to it at the end — don't lose time or confidence.

5. Plan around your semester. Using a break window to sort your licence keeps your long-term goals (like a Restricted Licence later) on track.

Next up: preparing for my Restricted Licence. 🚙
